The weed reproduces primarily from seed. Lavender to purple flowers are solitary on shoot tips and about the same size as diffuse knapweed flowers. Involucral bracts are stiff and black-tipped. The tip and upper bract margin have a soft, spine-like fringe and the center spine is shorter than others. Seeds are oval, brown to black with copious whitish bristles (pappus). Rosette leaves can be 6 inches long and deeply lobed. Leaves become smaller toward the top of the shoot and have smooth margins. Spotted knapweed forms one or more new shoots each year from a taproot. The shoots are branched and 1 to 3 feet tall. Spotted knapweed has a stout taproot. Seedlings have deeply divided leaves in a rosette.HabitatsSpotted knapweed occupies dry meadows, pastures, stony hills, roadsides, and the sandy or gravelly floodplains of streams and rivers. Currently eight flower-head insects and five root-boring insects have been approved and released for biological control of the weed in the U.S.Mechanical and Cultural ControlIf desirable grass competition is evident in spotted knapweed stands, judicious herbicide application that does not injure grasses may allow them to compete effectively with the weed. Irrigation (where possible) may help stimulate grass competition in these cases. However, infested rangeland or pastures often are degraded, allowing knapweed invasion, and herbicides alone will not restore the land to a productive state. Seeding suitable perennial grasses is necessary to prevent weed reinvasion. The weed reproduces primarily from seed. Lavender to purple flowers are solitary on shoot tips and about the same size as diffuse knapweed flowers. Involucral bracts are stiff and black-tipped. The tip and upper bract margin have a soft, spine-like fringe and the center spine is shorter than others. Seeds are oval, brown to black with copious whitish bristles (pappus). Rosette leaves can be 6 inches long and deeply lobed. Leaves become smaller toward the top of the shoot and have smooth margins. Spotted knapweed forms one or more new shoots each year from a taproot. The shoots are branched and 1 to 3 feet tall. Spotted knapweed has a stout taproot. Seedlings have deeply divided leaves in a rosette.HabitatsSpotted knapweed occupies dry meadows, pastures, stony hills, roadsides, and the sandy or gravelly floodplains of streams and rivers. Perennial plants resume growth in early spring and bolt at approximately the same time as diffuse knapweed. Flowering occurs through the summer into fall. Seed production can be as high as 3,700 seeds/foot2. Seed dispersal distances are generally short (less than 4 feet). Seeds can remain viable in the soil for several years.Management ApproachesSpotted knapweed is readily controlled with herbicides. However, it will reinvade unless cultural techniques are used.Biological ControlIt is believed it will take a complex of insects to reduce spotted knapweed populations. Currently eight flower-head insects and five root-boring insects have been approved and released for biological control of the weed in the U.S.Mechanical and Cultural ControlIf desirable grass competition is evident in spotted knapweed stands, judicious herbicide application that does not injure grasses may allow them to compete effectively with the weed. Irrigation (where possible) may help stimulate grass competition in these cases. However, infested rangeland or pastures often are degraded, allowing knapweed invasion, and herbicides alone will not restore the land to a productive state. Seeding suitable perennial grasses is necessary to prevent weed reinvasion. Livestock (sheep, goats, cattle) will eat spotted knapweed and can reduce its biomass and density.Chemical ControlChemical control is most effective when plants are in the seedling or rosette stage.
