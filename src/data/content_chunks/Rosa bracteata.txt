Overview
Appearance
Rosa bracteata
is an evergreen, thorny, climbing or trailing shrub that invades open, disturbed areas throughout the southern United States. Plants often grow in clumps. Stems are arching canes with recurved thorns.
Foliage
The alternate leaves are pinnately compound with serrated margins. Leaflets are 1-3 in. (2.5-7.6 cm) long.
Flowers
Flowers are white with five petals and occur in small clusters from April to June.
Fruit
Fruit are small green to red rose hips and are present from July to December.
Ecological Threat
Rosa bracteata
can form dense, impenetrable thickets in open forests and pastures. Infestations restrict cattle and wildlife use of land and displace native species. It is native to Asia and was first introduced into the United States as an ornamental.
