. Aphis citricidus only infests newly forming shoots. In southern Florida, new shoots generally last 10 days in summer and 10-16 days in autumn and spring. When the sprout tissues harden and become unsuitable for feeding, alate forms are differentiated for dispersal in search of other susceptible shoots for colonization. The nymphs can move from one tree to another covering a distance of up to 8-12 m. Alate adults, therefore, play an important role in the epidemiology of Citrus tristeza virus . In countries such as Argentina, Australia, Brazil, Kenya, Taiwan and Japan, two distinct population peaks per year are observed in the spring and autumn. A two-year field survey conducted in a citrus grove in southern Florida indicated that the population peaks did not fall on the same months each year, they were solely dependent on the rainfall of the preceding months ( Tsai, 1999 ). Spring and autumn flight peaks of alates of A. citricidus occur in South Africa ( Schwartz, 1965 ), Australia ( Carver, 1978 ) and Brazil ( Nickel et al., 1984 ). In Japan, brown citrus aphid populations peak three times per year but can be found on citrus in all seasons, except when overwintering ( Komazaki, 1993 ).  Sintomas: Young shoots are most susceptible to being infested by A. citricidus and support rapid population build-up. Like other aphids, A. citricidus is an external feeder and extracts sap from the host plant by penetrating its stylet into the phloem. Excess plant sap is excreted as honeydew which supports the growth of sooty mould. Heavily infested shoots are easily detected as colonies invade the entire axis. Ants attending the colonies are often present to pick up honeydew. When disturbed, A. citricidus populations sway rapidly in unison, making stridulatory movements with their hind legs presumably to fend off their enemies.

Aphis citricidus is ant-attended. The main environmental requirement for A. citricidus is the presence of plants of the genus Citrus that can support it nutritionally. The aphid can adapt to environmental conditions that differ from those of its place of origin (warm-humid climate).
 Sintomas: Young shoots are most susceptible to being infested by A. citricidus and support rapid population build-up. Like other aphids, A. citricidus is an external feeder and extracts sap from the host plant by penetrating its stylet into the phloem. Excess plant sap is excreted as honeydew which supports the growth of sooty mould. Heavily infested shoots are easily detected as colonies invade the entire axis. Ants attending the colonies are often present to pick up honeydew. When disturbed, A. citricidus populations sway rapidly in unison, making stridulatory movements with their hind legs presumably to fend off their enemies.

 Seccion ambiental: Biology and Ecology Genetics Based on morphological and molecular analysis, Lagos et al. (2014) reassigned Toxoptera to the genus Aphis , changing its status to subgenus. Further research using molecular methods has been conducted to discriminate A. citricidus from the other species in the subgenus, and especially from the related A. aurantii ( Wang and Qiao, 2009 ) . Reproductive Biology The biological behaviour of A. citricidus varies significantly depending on climatic conditions. Generally, it is anholocyclic (generations follow one another by parthenogenesis). In temperate regions of Japan, the production of the amphigonic forms and overwintering as a winter egg has been reported ( Komazaki et al., 1979 ) but this is exceptional. Physiology and Phenology Aphis citricidus only infests newly forming shoots. In southern Florida, new shoots generally last 10 days in summer and 10-16 days in autumn and spring. When the sprout tissues harden and become unsuitable for feeding, alate forms are differentiated for dispersal in search of other susceptible shoots for colonization. The nymphs can move from one tree to another covering a distance of up to 8-12 m. Alate adults, therefore, play an important role in the epidemiology of Citrus tristeza virus . In countries such as Argentina, Australia, Brazil, Kenya, Taiwan and Japan, two distinct population peaks per year are observed in the spring and autumn. A two-year field survey conducted in a citrus grove in southern Florida indicated that the population peaks did not fall on the same months each year, they were solely dependent on the rainfall of the preceding months ( Tsai, 1999 ). Spring and autumn flight peaks of alates of A. citricidus occur in South Africa ( Schwartz, 1965 ), Australia ( Carver, 1978 ) and Brazil ( Nickel et al., 1984 ). In Japan, brown citrus aphid populations peak three times per year but can be found on citrus in all seasons, except when overwintering ( Komazaki, 1993 ).
