 Reproduccion: Genetics Various cultivars have been developed for E. curvula , to improve its yield and palatability or tolerate droughts and low temperatures ( Busey and Wright, 1978 ; FAO, 2011 ; Cook et al., 2020 ). Environmental stress has been found to trigger genetic mutations, particularly from sexual to asexual apomictic reproduction ( Alagbo and Chauhan, 2023 ). Diploid (2n=20), polyploid and aneuploid (2n=63) types have been recorded. Chromosome numbers reported for E. curvula are 2n=20, 40, 50, 54, 60, 63, 70, 80, the base chromosome number being x=10. The diploids reproduce sexually while the polyploids are mostly pseudogamous diplosporous apomicts. Apomixis is mostly obligate, sometimes facultative. Sexual and apomictic types have been found in the tetraploids ( Cook et al., 2020 ). Germplasm collections are stored at various institutions ( Millennium Seed Bank, 2023 ; USDA-ARS, 2023 ). Reproductive Biology Eragrostis curvula reproduces via seeds ( PROTA, 2023 ). It does not reproduce vegetatively ( USDA-NRCS, 2023 ). Plants are very easy to establish from seed, having a prolific seed production ( Duke, 1983 ). Approximately 3,850,000 seeds or 110,000 spikelets are produced per kilogram. The seeds have a high degree of dormancy, surviving in the soil for several years ( McLaughlin and Bowers, 2006 ), with darkness inhibiting its germination by 79% ( Alagbo and Chauhan, 2023 ). Seeds take 20-25 days to germinate ( PROTA, 2023 ). Seedling vigour is excellent ( FAO, 2011 ), and their establishment is outstanding under semi-arid and arid environments ( Stalker and Wright, 1975 ). Physiology and Phenology Eragrostis curvula is a C4 grass well adapted to dry sandy loams ( FAO, 2011 ).  Reproduccion: Genetics Various cultivars have been developed for E. curvula , to improve its yield and palatability or tolerate droughts and low temperatures ( Busey and Wright, 1978 ; FAO, 2011 ; Cook et al., 2020 ). Environmental stress has been found to trigger genetic mutations, particularly from sexual to asexual apomictic reproduction ( Alagbo and Chauhan, 2023 ). Diploid (2n=20), polyploid and aneuploid (2n=63) types have been recorded. Chromosome numbers reported for E. curvula are 2n=20, 40, 50, 54, 60, 63, 70, 80, the base chromosome number being x=10. Reproductive Biology Eragrostis curvula reproduces via seeds ( PROTA, 2023 ). It does not reproduce vegetatively ( USDA-NRCS, 2023 ). Plants are very easy to establish from seed, having a prolific seed production ( Duke, 1983 ). Approximately 3,850,000 seeds or 110,000 spikelets are produced per kilogram. The seeds have a high degree of dormancy, surviving in the soil for several years ( McLaughlin and Bowers, 2006 ), with darkness inhibiting its germination by 79% ( Alagbo and Chauhan, 2023 ). Seeds take 20-25 days to germinate ( PROTA, 2023 ). Seedling vigour is excellent ( FAO, 2011 ), and their establishment is outstanding under semi-arid and arid environments ( Stalker and Wright, 1975 ). Physiology and Phenology Eragrostis curvula is a C4 grass well adapted to dry sandy loams ( FAO, 2011 ). Some cultivars are reported as helping with the control of nematodes in tea and tobacco plantations ( HeuzÃ© et al., 2015 ). The high concentration of pyrocatechol in the roots is suggested as the reason for the nematode control ( PROTA, 2023 ). The species grows well on poor soils but to improve the yield and palatability, the use of fertilizers is recommended ( Firn et al., 2010 ; FAO, 2011 ). for cold tolerance. Adapted to a wide range of habitats from moist to dry, hot to cold,

density by seedfall. Seed dispersed by water, contaminated equipment and soil, and
through planting. Occurs as dense colonies in old fields and along field margins and
right-of-ways, where it invades new forest plantations, open forests, forest openings and
special habitats like native prairies. Detrimental to wildlife, especially ground-nesting
