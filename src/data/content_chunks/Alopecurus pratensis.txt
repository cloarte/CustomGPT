However, the plant does have a short, non-aggressive root system and so pulling by hand or with tools may be possible.Flooding of the area does not seem to be an option because the grass readily endures periods of flooding by fresh or brackish water. A study in Iceland observed that the percentage ofAlopecurus geniculatusin a mixed grassland increased as the soil moisture increased.Alopecurus    geniculatuswas also found more often on bog soils than andosoils or sandy soils.[3]Alopecurus pratensisis somewhat drought tolerant however, if the soil moisture is low the grass remains short and the leaf tissue suffers heavily from desiccation. However, it was observed that these plants produced more seed heads.[4]Burning/Cutting/GrazingAlopecurus pratensishas been grown as a forage crop and so cutting and grazing may provide little control. As meadow foxtail matures, it becomes less palatable to cattle and other grasses tend to be eaten.[2]However, in Iceland early spring grazing decreased the percentage ofA. pratensisin a mixed species grass field.[3]Chemical ControlResearch done in the 1970s found that glyphosate (1.0 kg/ha) applied in August provided 100% reduction ofA. pratensisin a mixed sward.[5]Other research demonstrated that there was some toxicity toA. pratensisfrom dicamba.
