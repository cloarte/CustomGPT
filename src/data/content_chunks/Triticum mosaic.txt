BurrowsCauseTriticum mosaic virus (TriMV)SymptomsSymptoms of TriMV include yellowing of the leaf in a streaked or stippled pattern next to the leaf veins. Plants can be stunted if infected early in the season. Symptoms often appear first at the edges of a field or near patches of volunteer wheat. Symptoms may not appear until after spring warm-up. The virus can also be transmitted mechanically, but this is not considered the primary mechanism of movement through the field. When plants are co-infected with Wheat streak mosaic virus or High Plains virus, symptoms can be very severe and may lead to significant yield loss.Impact of DiseaseLittle is known about yield loss due to this virus, but yield loss due to other mite-transmitted viruses can range from insignificant to 100%. When the plant is infected early, heads may be sterile. Losses are generally more severe in years when the previous fall was abnormally warm or it was dry during the growing season. When plants are co-infected with Wheat streak mosaic virus or High Plains virus, symptoms can be very severe and may lead to significant yield loss.Life Cycle of the PathogenTriMV is transmitted by the wheat curl mite. The mite is so small that it cannot be seen with the naked eye, but when present in high numbers can make the wheat leaf curl so that the upper surface is rolled inward. Look inside a curled leaf with a 10x magnifying lens and you will see small white, cigar-shaped insects if the mite is present. Mites are dispersed up to two miles if environmental conditions are cool and humid. During hot weather they desiccate rapidly and wind dispersal is minimal.Both winter and spring wheat can be infected with TriMV. BurrowsCauseTriticum mosaic virus (TriMV)SymptomsSymptoms of TriMV include yellowing of the leaf in a streaked or stippled pattern next to the leaf veins. Plants can be stunted if infected early in the season. Symptoms often appear first at the edges of a field or near patches of volunteer wheat. Symptoms may not appear until after spring warm-up. The virus can also be transmitted mechanically, but this is not considered the primary mechanism of movement through the field. When the plant is infected early, heads may be sterile. Losses are generally more severe in years when the previous fall was abnormally warm or it was dry during the growing season. When plants are co-infected with Wheat streak mosaic virus or High Plains virus, symptoms can be very severe and may lead to significant yield loss.Life Cycle of the PathogenTriMV is transmitted by the wheat curl mite. The mite is so small that it cannot be seen with the naked eye, but when present in high numbers can make the wheat leaf curl so that the upper surface is rolled inward. Look inside a curled leaf with a 10x magnifying lens and you will see small white, cigar-shaped insects if the mite is present. Mites are dispersed up to two miles if environmental conditions are cool and humid. During hot weather they desiccate rapidly and wind dispersal is minimal.Both winter and spring wheat can be infected with TriMV. This virus was identified in 2006 in Wheat streak mosaic virus resistant wheat lines in Kansas.  biological control strategies have been developed for TriMV.Cultural Management PracticesNo varieties have shown consistent resistance to TriMV, however, some may be more tolerant than others.Winter wheat seeded early in the fall is more likely to become infected with TriMV. For spring wheat, early seeding reduces the risk of TriMV infections.The most important control measure is to destroy volunteer wheat and grassy weeds using herbicides or tillage. A three week period between volunteer control and crop emergence provides excellent control, since the mite cannot survive without green host tissue.Do not plant winter wheat next to late-maturing corn. Corn can also harbor the wheat curl mite.Important:When volunteer wheat is sprayed with herbicide, mites sense their host is dying and will move to a new field, so avoid spraying next to spring wheat during cool, moist and windy weather. When the weather is hot and dry, it is less likely the mite will survive to find a new host.Host Plant ResistanceChemical Management ProgramSeed and foliar insecticide applications have not proven effective for control of TriMV.Consult pesticide labels for additional rate, application, and safety information.
