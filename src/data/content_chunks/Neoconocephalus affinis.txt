adult males are 47-59 mm long, with pronotal length of 6.1-8.7 mm and hind femur length of 22-30 mm. Whereas females are 53-70 mm long, with pronotal length of 7.8-9.9 mm and hind femur 25-39 mm. The ovipositor is sword-shaped, 26-34 mm long; file 2.8-3.5 mm long, with 129-151 teeth evenly spaced. Head cone-shaped; the fastigium is elongate, 1.3-1.7 mm in males and 1.3-2.2 mm in females, often with a prominent ventral tooth. The mandible and lower labrum are bright orange (Walker and Greenfield, 1983). Anterior ventral carina of middle femur with three or four spines. Stridulatory vein in males, viewed from above, exceeding 3 mm and of approximate uniform width.

Male Neoconocephalus produce species-specific calls which females use to localize and approach males for mating ( Greenfield, 1990 ). Typically, among co-occurring species, calls differ distinctly in their temporal pattern, which allows females to selectively approach conspecific males ( Walker and Greenfield, 1983 ; Schul and Patterson, 2003 ; Deily and Schul, 2006 ). Neoconocephalus species go through three stages in their life cycle: egg, nymph and adult. Adult females use their ovipositor to open wounds in grasses, in between stems and sheaths of roots or leaves ( SINA, 2022b ). Some species are known to lay eggs on the ground and cracks in the bark ( Torres et al., 2008 ). Katydids protect the eggs by secreting a sticky, waterproof substance with which they cover and harden. In another species, Neoconocephalus triops , the eggs are light green in colour and are long and oval-shaped with pointed ends. In the conehead katydids, nymphs are light green, resembling the adults with long, slender bodies, but lack wings and sexual characteristics. At least in the species N. triops , nymphs go through seven instars ( SINA, 2022c ). Rather, they change from green to brown during late-instar moults, usually during the last moult ( SINA, 2022b ). Nymphs reach adulthood after about 7 weeks ( Whitesell, 1974 ). In other Neoconocephalus species, male calling is known to be energetically costly and it raises body temperature, especially inside the thorax. Thus, calling efficiency is tied to environmental temperature and physiological conditions of the male ( Heath and Josephson, 1970 ).
