Common mullein produces a basal rosette the first year and a single flowering stalk in the second year. The flowering stalk is stout, erect, and can reach up to 6 feet in height. The stem is longitudinally ridges by the bases of the leaves that continue down the stem beyond the point of attachment. The stem is densely wooly with branched hairs. Rosette leaves are covered with soft, fine hairs. Leaves on the flowering stalk are alternate, overlapping, light green, and densely wooly. Flowers are 1 inch in diameter, five lobed, and sulfur yellow in color. Flowers are grouped in a â€œcorn cobâ€-shaped flowering raceme. Common mullein produces many seed pods, each containing many small cylindrical seeds. The plant commonly grows in pastures, meadows, waste areas, and along fencerows and river bottoms.ImpactsCommon mullein is not considered palatable to livestock due to its woolliness. Common mullein is not considered a serious agricultural weed since it can be controlled by cultivation.  due to its woolliness. Common mullein is not considered a serious agricultural weed since it can be controlled by cultivation. Common mullein can be a hard plant to control due to prolific seed production, in particular in disturbed sites. However, common mullein is easily outcompeted in areas with a densely vegetated ground cover. It is an ephemeral plant, which is eventually displaced by other plants in undisturbed sites.Biology and EcologyCommon mullein reproduces by seed. Seeds germinate in early spring forming a rosette that continues to grow into late autumn and over winter. The following spring the plant produces a tall stem topped with a flowering raceme. Flowering and seed production occur from June to August. Individual plants can produce 100,000 to 180,000 seeds that can remain viable in the soil for 100 years.Management ApproachesIntegrated management should be aimed at preventing seed production and depleting the seed bank. A combination of herbicides or mechanical methods to remove rosettes and removal of seed heads from any plants that have bolted can control common mullein over time.Biological ControlA weevil (Gymnaetron tetrum) has been introduced to North America and is capable of reducing seed production by 50%.Mechanical and Cultural ControlSmall stands can be hand pulled prior to seeding. Common mullein can be cut or mowed to prevent seed production, but stems must be cut below the root crown to avoid rebolting.Chemical ControlA number of herbicides are available for control of common mullein.Examples of herbicides that can be used to manage common mulleinConsult herbicide labels for additional rate, application, and safety information. Common mullein produces a basal rosette the first year and a single flowering stalk in the second year. The flowering stalk is stout, erect, and can reach up to 6 feet in height. The stem is longitudinally ridges by the bases of the leaves that continue down the stem beyond the point of attachment. The stem is densely wooly with branched hairs. Rosette leaves are covered with soft, fine hairs. Leaves on the flowering stalk are alternate, overlapping, light green, and densely wooly. Flowers are 1 inch in diameter, five lobed, and sulfur yellow in color. Flowers are grouped in a â€œcorn cobâ€-shaped flowering raceme. Common mullein produces many seed pods, each containing many small cylindrical seeds. Seeds have numerous ridges and grooves.HabitatsCommon mullein prefers dry, gravely to sandy coarse textured soils. The plant commonly grows in pastures, meadows, waste areas, and along fencerows and river bottoms.ImpactsCommon mullein is not considered palatable to livestock due to its woolliness. Common mullein is not considered a serious agricultural weed since it can be controlled by cultivation. Common mullein can be a hard plant to control due to prolific seed production, in particular in disturbed sites.  due to its woolliness. Common mullein is not considered a serious agricultural weed since it can be controlled by cultivation. Common mullein can be a hard plant to control due to prolific seed production, in particular in disturbed sites. However, common mullein is easily outcompeted in areas with a densely vegetated ground cover. It is an ephemeral plant, which is eventually displaced by other plants in undisturbed sites.Biology and EcologyCommon mullein reproduces by seed. Seeds germinate in early spring forming a rosette that continues to grow into late autumn and over winter. The following spring the plant produces a tall stem topped with a flowering raceme. Flowering and seed production occur from June to August. Individual plants can produce 100,000 to 180,000 seeds that can remain viable in the soil for 100 years.Management ApproachesIntegrated management should be aimed at preventing seed production and depleting the seed bank. A combination of herbicides or mechanical methods to remove rosettes and removal of seed heads from any plants that have bolted can control common mullein over time.Biological ControlA weevil (Gymnaetron tetrum) has been introduced to North America and is capable of reducing seed production by 50%.Mechanical and Cultural ControlSmall stands can be hand pulled prior to seeding. Common mullein can be cut or mowed to prevent seed production, but stems must be cut below the root crown to avoid rebolting.Chemical ControlA number of herbicides are available for control of common mullein.Examples of herbicides that can be used to manage common mulleinConsult herbicide labels for additional rate, application, and safety information.
