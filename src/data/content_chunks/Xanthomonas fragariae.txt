 Invasion: Xanthomonas fragariaeis an aerobic, Gram-negative, non-spore-forming, non-capsulate rod, averaging 0.4 x 1.3 ¬µm in size. Most cells are non-motile, but some have a single polar flagellum. On beef extract peptone agar, or similar medium without added carbohydrate, colonies are circular, entire, convex, glistening, translucent to pale yellow (Bradbury, 1977). The bacterium spreads in symptomless infected strawberry plantlets and a method for detection of latent infections is necessary. It is a very slow-growing bacterium in culture and is easily overgrown by saprophytic bacteria (Moltmann and Zimmermann, 2005).

 Reproduccion: Residues of infected leaves and crown infections on runners used for planting are sources of inoculum for primary infections. The bacterium survives in the residues of infected leaves, in or on soil, from one crop to the next. In dried infected leaves, kept in the laboratory, the bacterium may survive for at least 2.5 years. Bacterial cells are transferred from residues to young leaves at the beginning of the growing season. From crown infection pockets, the bacterium causes lesions along the veins at the base of the youngest leaves, which develop in the apical crown region. X. fragariae is exuded from primary lesions, and bacterial cells are spread by aerosols, caused by rain and sprinkler irrigation, and transported by wind to healthy leaves. Penetration occurs through the stomata. Infections of the crown occur through local wounds or from affected leaves. During the growing season, several cycles of secondary infections may occur. The bacterium may attack flowers, but not fruits. During epidemics, when environmental conditions favour exudation and spread, X. fragariae may cause systemic infections associated with crown pockets. Systemic infections may arise under damp nursery conditions. X. fragariae is spread locally by splash dispersal. Commercial strawberry runners used for planting may also spread the bacterium. They may still bear old, whole or torn, infected leaves or have crown infection pockets. Moreover, almost invisible fragments of infected leaves may be hidden in the apical crown region or between the roots ( Kennedy and King, 1962a ). Reproductive Biology Xanthomonas fragariae colonizes leaf mesophyll, the top 1 cm portion of the petiole adjacent to the leaf blade, and is occasionally found colonizing xylem vessels down to the middle of the petioles ( Wang et al., 2018 ). Petiole tissue harbouring bacteria introduced either by direct entry through natural openings or wounds, or by systemic movement from infected foliar tissue, probably serves as a main source of initial inoculum in field plantings. The pathogen overwinters in soil on previously infected plant material and on live plants, including transplants. It can persist for extended periods on dry leaves even when buried into soil. Wolf et al. (2018) assessed the risks for infection of strawberry plants through dispersal of an aerosolized inoculum. Plant infections occurred when plants were up to 5 m distance from the inoculum source, but more frequently in plants wetted prior to inoculation than in plants kept dry. In glasshouse studies, it was shown that under conditions favourable for disease development, spray-inoculation of strawberry plants with estimated densities of X. fragariae as low as 2000 CFU per plant were able to cause symptoms. Pathogen aerosols can be formed during mowing of an infected crop or by water splashing on symptomatic plants during overhead irrigation or rain. Environmental Requirements Environmental conditions that favour X. fragariae infection are moderate to cool daytime temperatures (about 20¬∞C), low night-time temperatures and high humidity ( Maas, 1984 ).

 Sintomas: Typical symptoms of angular leaf spot on strawberry plants are small, water-soaked lesions on the lower surface of leaves. Generally, 1-4 mm, angular, shiny, water-soaked spots appear surrounded by the smallest veins. In the early stage, the spots are only visible on the lower surface and appear translucent against the light. They enlarge, coalesce and, after about 2 weeks, are also visible on the upper surface as water-soaked, angular spots, which become reddish brown in colour. They have a shiny appearance and are usually covered by bacterial exudate which, when dry, turns brown and appears as gum-like scales. Spots coalesce more frequently along the primary and secondary veins. The dead tissues tear and break off, and the diseased leaf may assume a ragged appearance. In the most severe cases, crown infection pockets may be seen inside after dissection. They appear as localized, water-soaked zones, frequently confined to one side of the crown. The bacterium has a systemic phase, probably resulting from infected transplants, and is characterized by dying of the crown. Typically, at this stage, there are signs of systemic invasion of adjacent stems and death of stems and leaflets ( Kennedy and King, 1962a ; Kennedy, 1965 ; Maas, 1984 ).

Reproductive Biology Xanthomonas fragariae colonizes leaf mesophyll, the top 1 cm portion of the petiole adjacent to the leaf blade, and is occasionally found colonizing xylem vessels down to the middle of the petioles ( Wang et al., 2018 ). Petiole tissue harbouring bacteria introduced either by direct entry through natural openings or wounds, or by systemic movement from infected foliar tissue, probably serves as a main source of initial inoculum in field plantings. The pathogen overwinters in soil on previously infected plant material and on live plants, including transplants. It can persist for extended periods on dry leaves even when buried into soil. Wolf et al. (2018) assessed the risks for infection of strawberry plants through dispersal of an aerosolized inoculum. Plant infections occurred when plants were up to 5 m distance from the inoculum source, but more frequently in plants wetted prior to inoculation than in plants kept dry. In glasshouse studies, it was shown that under conditions favourable for disease development, spray-inoculation of strawberry plants with estimated densities of X. fragariae as low as 2000 CFU per plant were able to cause symptoms. Pathogen aerosols can be formed during mowing of an infected crop or by water splashing on symptomatic plants during overhead irrigation or rain. Environmental Requirements Environmental conditions that favour X. fragariae infection are moderate to cool daytime temperatures (about 20¬∞C), low night-time temperatures and high humidity ( Maas, 1984 ).

 Sintomas: Typical symptoms of angular leaf spot on strawberry plants are small, water-soaked lesions on the lower surface of leaves. Generally, 1-4 mm, angular, shiny, water-soaked spots appear surrounded by the smallest veins. In the early stage, the spots are only visible on the lower surface and appear translucent against the light. They enlarge, coalesce and, after about 2 weeks, are also visible on the upper surface as water-soaked, angular spots, which become reddish brown in colour. They have a shiny appearance and are usually covered by bacterial exudate which, when dry, turns brown and appears as gum-like scales. Spots coalesce more frequently along the primary and secondary veins. The dead tissues tear and break off, and the diseased leaf may assume a ragged appearance. In the most severe cases, crown infection pockets may be seen inside after dissection. They appear as localized, water-soaked zones, frequently confined to one side of the crown. The bacterium has a systemic phase, probably resulting from infected transplants, and is characterized by dying of the crown. Typically, at this stage, there are signs of systemic invasion of adjacent stems and death of stems and leaflets ( Kennedy and King, 1962a ; Kennedy, 1965 ; Maas, 1984 ).

In dried infected leaves, kept in the laboratory, the bacterium may survive for at least 2.5 years. Bacterial cells are transferred from residues to young leaves at the beginning of the growing season. From crown infection pockets, the bacterium causes lesions along the veins at the base of the youngest leaves, which develop in the apical crown region. X. fragariae is exuded from primary lesions, and bacterial cells are spread by aerosols, caused by rain and sprinkler irrigation, and transported by wind to healthy leaves. Penetration occurs through the stomata. Infections of the crown occur through local wounds or from affected leaves. During the growing season, several cycles of secondary infections may occur. The bacterium may attack flowers, but not fruits. During epidemics, when environmental conditions favour exudation and spread, X. fragariae may cause systemic infections associated with crown pockets. Systemic infections may arise under damp nursery conditions. X. fragariae is spread locally by splash dispersal. Commercial strawberry runners used for planting may also spread the bacterium. They may still bear old, whole or torn, infected leaves or have crown infection pockets. Moreover, almost invisible fragments of infected leaves may be hidden in the apical crown region or between the roots ( Kennedy and King, 1962a ).
