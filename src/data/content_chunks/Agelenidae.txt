Boxelder bugCricketEarwigFleaHead liceMillipede & CentipedePaper wasp & YellowjacketSilverfish & FirebratSpringtailTickWoodliceHelpAuthors: Ashley Mueller, Buyung HadiSpiders are arachnids, and they are the primary arachnid in many habitats. They have eight legs and two body sections, the cephalothorax and the abdomen, which are separated by the waist. When young spiders hatch, they look like tiny adult spiders. They have eight legs and two body sections, the cephalothorax and the abdomen, which are separated by the waist. When young spiders hatch, they look like tiny adult spiders. As they grow, they molt to accommodate their developing bodies.Spiders are beneficial because they are predators of problematic insects. Although their natural habitat is outdoors, they can make their way indoors in search of prey. Most spiders have special glands that allow them to produce and manipulate silk, so they are able to capture prey.  They also have poison glands in their mandibles, allowing them to kill their prey. Generally, spiders are not able to pierce human skin with a bite, but if they are able to, the venom is harmless in most cases.IdentificationAdult grass spiderGrass spiders or funnel weavers are fast-moving spiders that can be identified by their use of funnel-shaped webs. Each web can be several inches wide and are visible at the tops of bushes or just above the soil. These predators sit inside the funnel and wait for prey. Grass spiders have long legs and narrow bodies, and they are commonly confused with the brown recluse spider. Depending on the species, grass spiders can have six or eight eyes, which are always arranged in two parallel rows. The color of grass spiders vary from white to black.HabitatGrass spider web measuring approximately 12 inches in diameterGrass spider webs are found on grass, weeds, and ground covers and in bushes and brush piles.  eyes, which are always arranged in two parallel rows. The color of grass spiders vary from white to black.HabitatGrass spider web measuring approximately 12 inches in diameterGrass spider webs are found on grass, weeds, and ground covers and in bushes and brush piles. Webs serve as a platform on which these spiders catch prey and a place where they can remain hidden. The web is not sticky; instead it has an arrangement of threads above it to hinder or divert insect flight. When insects fall onto the web, they are quickly captured by the spider.Grass spiders rarely leave their webs. In the fall, they may make their way indoors as they seek protection from the colder temperatures.Life Cycle and Seasonal HistoryFemale grass spiders deposit eggs sacs in the late summer and fall, and the eggs hatch in the spring. Spiderlings develop into adults in the late summer after a series of molts. Adults may enter buildings in the fall, but they do not overwinter in them.Human Interaction and Urban ImpactGenerally, grass spiders are not economically or medically important spiders. These spiders are beneficial because they feed on other insects. They may bite if mishandled, but they are not considered dangerous. Any medical questions or concerns related to a grass spider bite should be directed towards a physician.Prevention and Management ApproachesTo prevent spiders from entering homes and buildings, seal the foundation, inspect any window or door screens for holes, reduce clutter from around the foundation, and reduce any potential food sources for the spiders to keep them from entering or growing high numbers within the facility. Remove clutter which can serve as a hiding spot for spiders.If grass spider activity is observed indoors, remove spiders and egg sacs from the building and place outdoors or simply vacuum. Pesticides are not recommended because grass spiders are beneficial, and each spider would require direct application of the pesticide.Other Online ResourcesPenn State UniversityUniversity of KentuckyFor information regarding labels of chemical control options, please visitNPIC
