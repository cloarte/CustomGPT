Adult males and females will take nectar, and some are specially adapted for drinking from deep flowers using a long (sometimes very long) proboscis. However, most females are known for the habit of using their blade-like mandibles to slice into skin, drinking the blood that pools in the cut. Like other flies, they often need the blood-meal to aid in egg production, especially after the first brood. Females lay eggs on leaves and vegetation in small masses, usually near an aquatic or semiaquatic environment, though many also live in drier habitats. Larvae live in the soil, some growing to very large sizes (1-2 inches) over the course of six months to two years. Because the larvae are predatory, like many lower Brachycera (in between 1 and 2 on the tree above), they have venom to subdue prey. Large specimens are especially powerful, being able to capture larger victims like frogs and toads; this also means that they can inflict a painful bite if mishandled by a person. Because they are so diverse many have different habits, such as living in ant nests and some may even feed on plant material (though not confirmed and, thus, not economically important).Economic Importance:Horse and deer flies are most well known for their painful bites, usually received in meadows, at the beach or swimming pools in the summer (usually horse flies), or in woods, swamps and other outdoors type areas (usually deer flies). They are mostly large flies, thus their presence rarely goes unnoticed, causing psychological distress to people as well. They can also stress livestock and even drink so much blood to cause weakness or even death, when present in large numbers. Adult males and females will take nectar, and some are specially adapted for drinking from deep flowers using a long (sometimes very long) proboscis. However, most females are known for the habit of using their blade-like mandibles to slice into skin, drinking the blood that pools in the cut. Like other flies, they often need the blood-meal to aid in egg production, especially after the first brood. Females lay eggs on leaves and vegetation in small masses, usually near an aquatic or semiaquatic environment, though many also live in drier habitats. Because the larvae are predatory, like many lower Brachycera (in between 1 and 2 on the tree above), they have venom to subdue prey. Large specimens are especially powerful, being able to capture larger victims like frogs and toads; this also means that they can inflict a painful bite if mishandled by a person. Because they are so diverse many have different habits, such as living in ant nests and some may even feed on plant material (though not confirmed and, thus, not economically important).Economic Importance:Horse and deer flies are most well known for their painful bites, usually received in meadows, at the beach or swimming pools in the summer (usually horse flies), or in woods, swamps and other outdoors type areas (usually deer flies). They are mostly large flies, thus their presence rarely goes unnoticed, causing psychological distress to people as well. They can also stress livestock and even drink so much blood to cause weakness or even death, when present in large numbers.
