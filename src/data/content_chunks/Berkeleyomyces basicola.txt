 Invasion: Berkeleyomyces basicolaforms grey, grey-brown, olivaceous-brown, dark-brown and even black colonies on nutrient media. Cultural appearance among isolates can vary significantly as reported byStover (1950)andNel et al. (2019)with isolates often being separated into grey and brown types. Additionally, in some colonies, albinism can be observed due to a mutation in the melanin biosynthesis gene. This results in the appearance of purely white sections in cultures (Nel et al., 2019). Microscopically these structures are identical to ones produced in coloured cultures, except for the complete lack of brown pigmentation in hyphae and chlamydospores.

Briefly, in the presence of host root exudates, inoculum in the soil will germinate to produce a germ tube that grows and develops until encountering a host. The pathogen may penetrate the host through the cell wall of epidermal cells, root hairs or by wounds by means of a penetration peg ( Nan et al., 1992 ; Hood and Shew, 1997a ) or appressoria-like structures ( Baard and Laubscher, 1985 ; Mauk and Hine, 1988 ). Colonization occurs both by intra- and intercellular growth of the fungal hyphae ( Punja et al., 1992 ). Once in the cell lumen the host plasma membrane is invaginated by the hyphae. Hyphae grow as sickle-shaped cells that are constricted at the septa and eventually fill most of the cell. Hyphae then penetrate adjacent cells. When entering the necrotrophic phase of the pathogen life cycle, lesions become apparent and both endoconidia and chlamydospores are produced abundantly. Generally, the pathogen colonizes epidermal and cortical cells and only rarely is it found in the vascular tissue. As the periderm is formed, the diseased cortical tissue may slough off.  Sintomas: It is often difficult to identify plants having black root rot by above-ground symptoms. The most common symptom is stunting. With severe root damage, plants may exhibit chlorosis and wilting. A specific replant problem for cherry ( Prunus ) and plum ( Prunus domestica ) has been associated with B. basicola ( Sewell and Wilson, 1975 ). Plants are rarely killed from black root rot. Symptoms start out as brown to black discoloured lesions on the root. Disease quickly increases until much of the root system may take on a black discolouration. The cortical tissue collapses and the epidermis and cortical tissue may slough off. Vascular invasion often does not occur and peeling back the black discoloured tissue should reveal a white, healthy vascular cylinder. Root systems may have few lateral roots or have stubby roots. Disease symptoms on some crops may extend to the stem below the soil surface. Disease identification can be easily accomplished through magnification of the root tissue to observe the presence of the chlamydospores of the pathogen, which are present within a few days of infection.

 Sintomas: It is often difficult to identify plants having black root rot by above-ground symptoms. The most common symptom is stunting. With severe root damage, plants may exhibit chlorosis and wilting. A specific replant problem for cherry ( Prunus ) and plum ( Prunus domestica ) has been associated with B. basicola ( Sewell and Wilson, 1975 ). Plants are rarely killed from black root rot. Symptoms start out as brown to black discoloured lesions on the root. Disease quickly increases until much of the root system may take on a black discolouration. The cortical tissue collapses and the epidermis and cortical tissue may slough off. Vascular invasion often does not occur and peeling back the black discoloured tissue should reveal a white, healthy vascular cylinder. Root systems may have few lateral roots or have stubby roots. Disease symptoms on some crops may extend to the stem below the soil surface. Disease identification can be easily accomplished through magnification of the root tissue to observe the presence of the chlamydospores of the pathogen, which are present within a few days of infection.

 Seccion ambiental: Biology and Ecology Genetics In South Africa, populations of B. basicola isolates appear to be largely clonal ( Geldenhuis et al., 2006 ). In other regions of the world, such as the USA, Europe, Australia and British Columbia, higher genetic diversity among isolates has been found ( Punja and Sun, 1999 ; Nehl et al., 2004 ; Geldenhuis et al., 2006 ). Although lower levels of genetic diversity are found in areas of introduction, the origin of B. basicola is still uncertain. Reproductive Biology According to genomic analyses, B. basicola has a heterothallic mating system ( Nel et al., 2018b ). Although both mating types can be found in nature, no sexual state has been described for B. basicola. Associations Berkeleyomyces basicola is not typically associated with symbionts, but disease incidence and severity can be worse in fields infested with both B. basicola and the root knot nematode Meloidogyne incognita ( Walker et al., 1999 ; Wheeler et al., 2000 ). Root damage induced by the nematodes allows easy infection by B. basicola . Typical yield reduction is much worse when pathogen or pest are found together compared to when either is found on their own. In addition to soil, the pathogen has been detected in peat, which has been demonstrated to be a source of B. basicola in the cause of black root rot on citrus seedlings ( Graham and Timmer, 1991 ). Movement of plant material is an important factor in long-range dissemination, especially for transplants and container-grown plants. The pathogen has been demonstrated to be seedborne in groundnut ( Arachis hypogaea ) ( Labuschagne and Kotze, 1991 ). In closed greenhouse production systems, spores have been detected in the air, indicating another possible inoculum source ( Graham and Timmer, 1991 ).
