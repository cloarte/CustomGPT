 Termite from an AntOften termites are confused with ants, but may be separated based on differences in antennae, overall body shape, and wings.Antennae:Ants have elongate, elbowed antennae that may or may not have a club. Termites have straight beadlike antennae always without clubs.Body Shape:Ants have a "wasp-waste," the abdomen narrows where it attaches to the thorax.  Termite from an AntOften termites are confused with ants, but may be separated based on differences in antennae, overall body shape, and wings.Antennae:Ants have elongate, elbowed antennae that may or may not have a club. Termites have straight beadlike antennae always without clubs.Body Shape:Ants have a "wasp-waste," the abdomen narrows where it attaches to the thorax. Termites have a broad attachment between the thorax and abdomen.Wings:The front and hind wings of ants are different in size and shape. Additionally ants have a dark area (stigma) about 2/3 from the base on the front edge of the forewing. Termite front and back wings are the same size and shape, additionally they lack a stigma.Pest TermitesIn the U.S. economically important termites are broadly placed in three categories; subterranean, drywood, and dampwood.Dampwood TermitesDampwood termites require wood with high moisture content and colonies are entirely wood dwelling. Dampwood termites tend to be larger than subterranean termites with workers and soldiers 13-20 mm (1/2-3/4 in) long, and winged reproductives may be 25 mm (1 in) long. The entire colony lives within dry sound wood, and may attack structural timbers, woodwork, and even furniture. OrderIsopteraFamilyRhinotermitidaeGenusReticulitermesScientific NameReticulitermes flavipesCommon Nameeastern subterranean termiteSubterranean TermitesThese termites are generally associated to some extent with the ground. They may excavate and maintain passages through the soil and will construct shelter tubes to cross inhospitable territory (such as concrete), to reach above ground food sources. Generally subterranean termites must return to the soil for moisture, but occasionally when conditions are suitable, such as near a leak, they may remain and even reproduce aboveground.Subterranean termites prefer to only eat spring wood, leaving infested wood with a hollowed, layered or channeled appearance. They do not eject pellets, powder, or other fragments for their feeding galleries like other termites.Subterranean termite colonies may be small with 50,000 individuals foraging over 20 m2, to large with 5 million individuals foraging over 1300 m2 (1/3 the area of an American football field). Colonies are not centralized but may have several nesting and foraging sites. Thus, a barrier treatment of pesticides used to keep termites from a building may have no effect on the rest of the colony outside the treatment zone.TheEastern Subterranean Termite(Reticulitermes flavipes(Kollar)) andFormosan Subterranean Termite(Coptotermes formosanusShiraki) represent the two most economically important subterranean termite species in North America.Eastern Subterranean TermiteTheeastern subterranean termite(Reticulitermes flavipes(Kollar)) is the most common and wide spread termite in North America. eastern subterranean termite(Reticulitermes flavipes(Kollar)) is the most common and wide spread termite in North America. Winged adults are relatively small, 10 mm (3/8 in) long, have milky translucent wings, and a brownish to black body. Swarms occur in the spring, but in heated buildings may occur even in winter. Colonies may consist of between 50,000 and 5 million individuals. Generally the eastern subterranean termite must maintain contact with the ground for moisture. They can be easily discovered by the presence of shelter tubes.Formosan Subterranean TermiteTheFormosan subterranean termite(Coptotermes formosanusShiraki) is native to mainland China, but has been spread throughout the world by human activity. Currently the Formosan subterranean termite is found across the southern U.S. and is spreading inward, but winter temperatures will probably halt permanent establishment beyond a northern limit. This species is larger than most other subterranean termites, winged reproductives are 12-15 mm (1/2 in), the body is a pale yellowish brown, and the wings are densely covered in tiny hairs.
