 Reproduccion: Genetics The chromosome number of O. ramosa is 2n=24. In addition to the genetic variability, evident from the range of varieties and sub-species noted above, there is variation between morphologically similar populations associated with particular hosts. Brault et al. (2007) demonstrated a significant variation in the virulence of parasites collected from rapeseed, tobacco and hemp in France. This variation suggests a development of host-related pathotypes, which was confirmed by differences in RAPD analysis. Musselman and Parker (1982) showed similar variation in the host preference of forms attacking Solanaceae and lettuce. Microsatellite markers in O. ramosa developed by Corre et al. (2014) are potentially useful for population genetics studies and for elucidating host-associated genetic divergence in the species. Random amplified polymorphic DNA (RAPD) and inter simple sequence repeat (ISSR) markers have also been used to determine the taxonomic relationships among Orobanche species and have demonstrated the close relationship between O. ramosa and O. mutelii ( Sharawy and Karakish, 2015 ). Reproductive Biology When the flowers of O. ramosa open, the mature anthers are already in contact with the stigma, and self-pollination is apparently the norm ( Musselman et al., 1982 ). Seeds are produced in very large numbers, many hundreds per capsule, and may remain viable in the soil for many years, possibly for 10 years or more, and certainly for 5 years in many situations. A detailed study of the reproductive biology of O. ramosa subsp. mutelii [ Orobanche mutelli ] was conducted by Prider (2015) in South Australia. They found that the key characters contributing to the success of the plant include self-pollination, rapid maturation of the reproductive stages and high seed output. Phenology and Physiology Orobanche ramosa is an obligate parasite, needing to connect to a host root within a few days of germination. The seed is minute (approximately 0.2 x 0.4 mm), from which only the radicle emerges, and this can grow only a few millimetres long. A chemical stimulus is needed to trigger Orobanche germination, normally coming from host roots, therefore, Orobanche normally germinates only when a host root is nearby. A moist environment is required for several days, together with suitable temperatures, before the mature seed is responsive to germination stimulants. This preparatory period is known as conditioning or preconditioning. Conditioned seeds remain responsive to germination stimulants for a limited period, after which secondary dormancy may be induced. Their ability to respond to germination stimuli fades gradually when the seeds dry, and they remain dormant until re-conditioned ( Timko et al., 1989 ; Joel et al., 1995a ). The effects of different temperature and moisture regimes on the germination and viability of O. aegyptiaca have been reported by Kebreab and Murdoch (1999a) . These results are likely to be applicable to O. ramosa . The optimum temperatures for conditioning and germination of O. ramosa are between 18¬∞C and 23¬∞C.  Invasion: Orobanche ramosaproduces leafless flowering stems, 15-20(-30) cm high, usually very branched, bearing alternate scales, less than 1 cm long. The plant is pale, completely lacking chlorophyll. The base of the stem, below ground, is normally swollen and tuberous. The rest of the stem is usually slender, simple or branched, and pale yellow. Leaves are scale-like.  Reproduccion: Genetics The chromosome number of O. ramosa is 2n=24. In addition to the genetic variability, evident from the range of varieties and sub-species noted above, there is variation between morphologically similar populations associated with particular hosts. Brault et al. (2007) demonstrated a significant variation in the virulence of parasites collected from rapeseed, tobacco and hemp in France. This variation suggests a development of host-related pathotypes, which was confirmed by differences in RAPD analysis. Musselman and Parker (1982) showed similar variation in the host preference of forms attacking Solanaceae and lettuce. Microsatellite markers in O. ramosa developed by Corre et al. (2014) are potentially useful for population genetics studies and for elucidating host-associated genetic divergence in the species. Random amplified polymorphic DNA (RAPD) and inter simple sequence repeat (ISSR) markers have also been used to determine the taxonomic relationships among Orobanche species and have demonstrated the close relationship between O. ramosa and O. mutelii ( Sharawy and Karakish, 2015 ). Reproductive Biology When the flowers of O. ramosa open, the mature anthers are already in contact with the stigma, and self-pollination is apparently the norm ( Musselman et al., 1982 ). Seeds are produced in very large numbers, many hundreds per capsule, and may remain viable in the soil for many years, possibly for 10 years or more, and certainly for 5 years in many situations. A detailed study of the reproductive biology of O. ramosa subsp. mutelii [ Orobanche mutelli ] was conducted by Prider (2015) in South Australia. They found that the key characters contributing to the success of the plant include self-pollination, rapid maturation of the reproductive stages and high seed output. Phenology and Physiology Orobanche ramosa is an obligate parasite, needing to connect to a host root within a few days of germination. The seed is minute (approximately 0.2 x 0.4 mm), from which only the radicle emerges, and this can grow only a few millimetres long. A chemical stimulus is needed to trigger Orobanche germination, normally coming from host roots, therefore, Orobanche normally germinates only when a host root is nearby. A moist environment is required for several days, together with suitable temperatures, before the mature seed is responsive to germination stimulants. This preparatory period is known as conditioning or preconditioning. Conditioned seeds remain responsive to germination stimulants for a limited period, after which secondary dormancy may be induced. Their ability to respond to germination stimuli fades gradually when the seeds dry, and they remain dormant until re-conditioned ( Timko et al., 1989 ; Joel et al., 1995a ). The effects of different temperature and moisture regimes on the germination and viability of O. aegyptiaca have been reported by Kebreab and Murdoch (1999a) . These results are likely to be applicable to O. ramosa . The optimum temperatures for conditioning and germination of O. ramosa are between 18¬∞C and 23¬∞C. Environmental requirements Research on soil and bioclimatic factors that influence the interaction between host plants and O. ramosa has shown that the level of O. ramosa infestation correlates negatively with the pH and total humidity index, and correlates positively with the organic matter content ( Lyra et al., 2016 ). The same study found that soil and climatic variables together explained 63% of infestation variance. The effects of temperature, oxygen concentration and water potential of the medium on O. ramosa seed germination have been investigated by Gibot-Leclerc et al. (2004) .
