Population Size and Density As the cotton aphid is a species native to warm-temperate, subtropical and tropical geographic areas, it develops anholocyclically, remaining active throughout the year with generations of parthenogenetic females following one after another without interruption. Populations that develop in spring on the shoots of trees (e.g. citrus or hibiscus) develop a very high number of individuals in about 20 days. When the colonies become very overcrowded, they begin to differentiate alate that leave the shoots in search of new ones ( Graham, 1968 ). The production of alates is promoted by nutritional factors and crowding. However, there is no conclusive evidence demonstrating the relative importance of nutrition versus crowding in alate formation in A. gossypii . When no new shoots are available to colonize, the winged forms take flight and, taking advantage of the air currents, go in search of new host plants where they can spend the summer and autumn. In winter, the biological cycle slows down as temperatures drop and the colonies, consisting of only a few aphids, move to host plants that can provide food. In horticultural plants, the development of generations follows the plant cycle and colonies are more or less numerous depending on food availability.  Sintomas: Infestations of A. gossypii do not cause major deformation in plants. In citrus trees, the leaves do not suffer distortion or wilting. However, the shoots may show more limited development, and greater fruit drop may be observed which, in years of excessive fruit set, may be beneficial to the development of the remaining fruit. The greatest damage can occur in young plants (4 years old) as strong infestations can cause a delay in development. In some ornamental plants (e.g. Hibiscus ), heavy infestations can cause desiccation of plant parts, yellowing and premature leaf drop, and general dulling of the plant because of the sooty mould that develops on the honeydew produced by the aphids. Plants of the Cucurbitaceae can become stunted, and the stems twisted. In some cases (i.e. pomegranate) colonies can settle on the fruit causing discolouration of the peel.

Population Size and Density As the cotton aphid is a species native to warm-temperate, subtropical and tropical geographic areas, it develops anholocyclically, remaining active throughout the year with generations of parthenogenetic females following one after another without interruption. Populations that develop in spring on the shoots of trees (e.g. citrus or hibiscus) develop a very high number of individuals in about 20 days. When the colonies become very overcrowded, they begin to differentiate alate that leave the shoots in search of new ones ( Graham, 1968 ). The production of alates is promoted by nutritional factors and crowding. However, there is no conclusive evidence demonstrating the relative importance of nutrition versus crowding in alate formation in A. gossypii . When no new shoots are available to colonize, the winged forms take flight and, taking advantage of the air currents, go in search of new host plants where they can spend the summer and autumn. In winter, the biological cycle slows down as temperatures drop and the colonies, consisting of only a few aphids, move to host plants that can provide food. In horticultural plants, the development of generations follows the plant cycle and colonies are more or less numerous depending on food availability. In protected environments, A. gossypii can develop faster and the number of generations can increase considerably.  Sintomas: Infestations of A. gossypii do not cause major deformation in plants. In citrus trees, the leaves do not suffer distortion or wilting. However, the shoots may show more limited development, and greater fruit drop may be observed which, in years of excessive fruit set, may be beneficial to the development of the remaining fruit. The greatest damage can occur in young plants (4 years old) as strong infestations can cause a delay in development. In some ornamental plants (e.g. Hibiscus ), heavy infestations can cause desiccation of plant parts, yellowing and premature leaf drop, and general dulling of the plant because of the sooty mould that develops on the honeydew produced by the aphids. Plants of the Cucurbitaceae can become stunted, and the stems twisted. In some cases (i.e. pomegranate) colonies can settle on the fruit causing discolouration of the peel.

Reproductive Biology Aphis gossypii carries out an anholocycle through uninterrupted generations of parthenogenetic females with colonies remaining active throughout the year, often on different host plants ( Moran, 1992 ). Physiology and Phenology In warm-temperate climates, the annual cycle of A. gossypii occurs on different host plants that the species alternates between in different seasons, mainly driven by the need to find plants that provide food. Populations generally reach a peak in numbers during the spring, as there is greater availability of shoots and environmental conditions are optimal. During the other seasons, populations are reduced due to lower food availability, high temperatures and often dry summer weather, and the activity of numerous natural enemies that drastically reduce colony numbers. Colonies may increase again in the autumn, but infestations are rather modest. Activity Patterns Some populations of A. gossypii may function as distinct species ( Blackman and Eastop, 2017 ). Great specialization stems from this, leading to the development of behaviours and physiological adaptations that should reflect genetic recombination.
