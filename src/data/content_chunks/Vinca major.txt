Although both species are grown in cultivation, onlyV. majorhas established itself as a weed.  The characteristics ofV. majorthat distinguish it from other California species are its milky latex, dark green leaves, and periwinkle colored flowers. It has been cultivated in areas of the U.S. with mild or temperate climates where it has since naturalized. InV. major2n=92 and inV. minor2n=46 (Stearn 1973). Apparently such polyploidy is frequently associated with rampant vegetative growth and poor reproduction by seed (Salisbury 1961). This creates a problem where it is competing in areas with native flora (McClintock 1985). It appears to be quite stable in the environment; dry or cold weather may temporarily set growth back, butVincaquickly resprouts and regains lost ground coverage.Vinca majorrequires active management to control and/or eliminate it.Rate of spread is not known from the literature, but usually it spreads only from the point of planting along shady corridors.Former preserve managers at TNC's Santa Rosa Plateau and Ring Mountain preserves in California indicated that the extent ofV. majoron those properties has been increasing.
