 Reproduccion: The chromosome number reported for H. annuus is 2 n = 34 ( Flora of North America Editorial Committee, 2018 ). This species was domesticated in North America, and during the last 3000 years, Native American Indians increased the seed size by approximately 1000 percent.  Seccion ambiental: Biology and Ecology Genetics The chromosome number reported for H. annuus is 2 n = 34 ( Flora of North America Editorial Committee, 2018 ). This species was domesticated in North America, and during the last 3000 years, Native American Indians increased the seed size by approximately 1000 percent. They gradually changed the genetic composition of the plant by repeatedly selecting the largest seeds ( USDA-NRCS, 2018 ). Gene flow among wild and domesticated H. annuus has been recorded ( Burke et al., 2002 ; Gutierrez et al., 2014 ; USDA-NRCS, 2018 ). In Mexico, this species produces flowers irregularly across the year ( Vibrans, 2011 ). Activity Patterns In H. annuus , the growth cycle is usually about 4 months, but it ranges from 75-180 days, depending on the environment and genotype. Sowing to seedling emergence takes 5-10 days, emergence to floral initiation 15-20 days, floral initiation to first flowering 20-90 days, flowering 5-15 days and flowering to seed maturity 30-45 days. Pronounced heliotropism is a characteristic of sunflowers. Young heads and leaves face east in the morning and then they follow the movement of the sun to face west in the evening. Anthesis progresses from the periphery of the head inwards at 1-4 rows of florets per day. Anthesis of a floret starts early in the morning and the stigma becomes fully extended and receptive the following morning. Fertilization is complete by the evening of the second day. At physiological maturity (30-40 days after last anthesis) the head becomes yellow, the bracts brown and about 75% of the leaves are desiccated. During the following 10 days, the seed will dry to 10-12% moisture content. Seeds of H. annuu s show dormancy. Some seeds will germinate in the next growing season, but others may remain up to 3 years buried in the soil waiting for suitable environmental conditions ( Duke, 1983 ; Vossen and Umali, 2001 ; PROTA, 2018 ; USDA-NRCS, 2018 ).
