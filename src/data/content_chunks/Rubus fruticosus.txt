Roots are stout, branched, creeping underground, growing vertically to a maximum depth of 1.5 m, depending on the soil type, from a woody crown up to 20 cm in diameter. The fruit is an aggregated berry, 10-20 mm long, changing colour from green to red to black as it ripens, made up of approximately 20 to 50 single-seeded drupelets. Seeds are deeply and irregularly pitted, oval, coloured light to dark brown, and 2.6-3.7 mm long and 1.6-2.5 mm wide.

 Reproduccion: Physiology and Phenology Seeds in the soil seedbank may sometimes remain viable for years. For example, seeds of Rubus anglocandicans , a member of R. fruticosus aggregate, were still viable after being buried in the soil for 5 years ( Delaisse et al., 2023 ). Seeds require stratification and germinate in spring. Seedlings are poor competitors, but this is compensated by the large number of seeds produced annually. Shading and competition affect seedling survival negatively and most seedlings die in early establishment; Amor (1971) found that only 15% of seedlings at one study site survived the first year. Those which succeed in establishing can grow up to four canes, with a length of up to 1 m, producing daughter plants in their first autumn. Blackberries may also vary in their deciduous nature. Evergreen types often have canes which persist for more than 2 years, new laterals being produced each year. In Australia, it is restricted to temperate climates with an annual rainfall of at least 700 mm, and occurs at any altitude ( Bruzzese, 1998 ).
