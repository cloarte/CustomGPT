The J2 congregate at the meristem of infested tillers and wait for ovary initiation before forming a gall by invading the ovary and modifying its development. Within the gall, the J2 feeds and moults through two more juvenile stages (third stage juvenile or J3 and fourth-stage juvenile or J4) to adults (females and males). From the third (second parasitic juvenile or PJ2) moult onwards, there was greater variability in the size of the female nematodes than in the males and sexual dimorphism became very pronounced. The transition from the DJ2 to the second-stage parasitic juvenile (PJ2) is marked by the disappearance of the numerous lipid storage granules which are characteristic of the DJ2, and the development in the PJ2 of an intestine which becomes more pronounced in each succeeding stage, particularly in the adult female. In A. agrostis the DJ2 has the thickest cuticle of all stages, including adults. Fourth-stage juveniles and adult males have thicker cuticles than the females at the same stages of development. Moulting appears to involve resorption of the innermost basal zone of the shed cuticle as well as morphological and chemical changes to the epicuticle ( Bird and Stynes, 1981c ). A gall of A. agrostis may be occupied by a few to 20 adults where sexual reproduction results in several hundred eggs per gall ( Riley and Bertozzi, 2004 ). The nematodes develop to J2 in the eggs, which hatch in the gall. These freshly hatched J2 cannot survive desiccation, but mature physiologically to become the survival stage (DJ2), as the host dies and desiccates in the spring or early autumn ( Riley and Bertozzi, 2004 ).  Sintomas: Anguina agrostis causes no obvious symptoms on grass shoots except that of the inflorescence which is so distorted that the parasitized plants were once described as a new species, Agrostis sylvatica [ Agrostis capillaris ]. Symptoms of the inflorescence include pronounced lengthening of glumes and paleae, reaching two to three times normal length, and elongated flower galls which are modified ovaries that look greenish or purple and may be 4-5 mm long. Lodicules, stamens and sometimes other flower parts are suppressed in such parasitized flowers ( Goodey, 1930 ). Adult nematodes (1-3 females, 1-3 males) are found within these galls. Later, the females lay eggs and die, the eggs hatch and the second-stage juveniles fill the gall which then turns into a seed gall. Seed galls containing the nematodes are dark brown. They may look similar to normal seeds but are lighter and hence can be separated mechanically from them.

A gall of A. agrostis may be occupied by a few to 20 adults where sexual reproduction results in several hundred eggs per gall ( Riley and Bertozzi, 2004 ). The nematodes develop to J2 in the eggs, which hatch in the gall. These freshly hatched J2 cannot survive desiccation, but mature physiologically to become the survival stage (DJ2), as the host dies and desiccates in the spring or early autumn ( Riley and Bertozzi, 2004 ).  Sintomas: Anguina agrostis causes no obvious symptoms on grass shoots except that of the inflorescence which is so distorted that the parasitized plants were once described as a new species, Agrostis sylvatica [ Agrostis capillaris ]. Symptoms of the inflorescence include pronounced lengthening of glumes and paleae, reaching two to three times normal length, and elongated flower galls which are modified ovaries that look greenish or purple and may be 4-5 mm long. Lodicules, stamens and sometimes other flower parts are suppressed in such parasitized flowers ( Goodey, 1930 ). Adult nematodes (1-3 females, 1-3 males) are found within these galls. Later, the females lay eggs and die, the eggs hatch and the second-stage juveniles fill the gall which then turns into a seed gall. Seed galls containing the nematodes are dark brown. They may look similar to normal seeds but are lighter and hence can be separated mechanically from them.
