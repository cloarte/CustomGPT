In heavily grazed grasslands, however, the reduced competition between shrubs and grasses may negate any positive effect burning had on shrub control. However, carbohydrate reserves are at a maximum in the fall.[1]MANAGEMENT/MONITORINGManagement Requirements:Although sumacs are native North American taxa, they can present distinct problems in wetlands, prairies, and rangeland.  Sumac encroachment in bogs has been encouraged by nitrate and orthophosphate laden runoff from adjacent farmland[2]and is shading out characteristic bog species.[3]Aldous (1934)[1]discusses the spread ofRhus glabrain native prairies in KS.  Sumac dominance in rangelands has been shown to increase under heavy grazing pressure[4]and under prescribe burning management.[5]Rhus glabrais susceptible to a number of control practices.  Cutting for 2 or 3 succesive years shortly after flowering (late spring early summer) can help control the spread of sumac since this is the time when carbohydrate reserves are the lowest and the species has a reduced capacity to respond to top removal.[6][7]Kline (1982) demonstrated that cutting 5 times in a period of 2 yrs reduced sumac density by 2/3 (compared to a control).  Cutting can also be used in combination with herbicides or prescribed burns.  Waller (1982)[8]suggests cutting to control young (<5 yrs) stems and cutting and treating with glyphosate to control older stems. In heavily grazed grasslands, however, the reduced competition between shrubs and grasses may negate any positive effect burning had on shrub control.  Because sumac and perennial grass dominants reserve carbohydrates are depleted and stored at the same time, prescribed burns to favor one will also favor the other.
