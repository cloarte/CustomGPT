(Photo by Tom Murray)Biology and Behavior:Skin beetles (many also known as carpet beetles) are a relatively small group of common beetles, with about 700 species worldwide. As larvae most scavenge dried materials, especially those rich in protein. Many feed on dead insects or dried plant materials. They are also often found associated with late-stage, decaying carcasses that are somewhat dried. There they feed on most remaining tissues except bone. Some skin beetle larvae are associated with various nests, where they feed on dead individuals and food stores (bee and wasp nests), feathers (bird nests), or fur (mammal nests). Some are associated with the egg cases of praying mantises or spiders, while a few species are predators of the larvae and eggs of bees and wasps (and eggs of spiders).

Carpet beetles (Anthrenusspp.) and others that live in our homes may feed on animal products including wool fabrics and carpets, furs, leather, and silk. Other Dermestidae are know to feed on our stored grains and products, such as rice, oats, beans, corn, and others. (Photo by Tom Murray)Biology and Behavior:Ladybugs or lady beetles, and bean/squash beetles comprise a group of almost 6,000 species worldwide. The majority of ladybugs feed on soft-bodied insects as larvae and adults. Prey may include scale insects, mealybugs, aphids, whiteflies and mites. A few species feed on pollen, while one group (Epilachninae[15]) feeds on living plant tissue. Rarely larvae are found in ant or termite nests. Many ladybugs are brightly-colored to warn that they are distasteful or toxic. In fact many are known to exhibit reflexive bleeding, where they exude sticky, noxious liquids from their leg joints to deter predators. Ladybugs may form large masses made up of hundreds to thousands of individuals when overwintering or dormant in warm weather.  so (including human structures).Economic Importance:Many ladybugs are important control agents of plant-feeding insects, particularly pests such as sucking bugs (Hemiptera: Sternorrhyncha) and certain mites. Adults and larvae are voracious and can decimate populations of these pests. For this reason they are sometimes imported from other parts of the world for biocontrol of invasive insects, and the most famous example is the vedalia beetle (Rodolia cardinalis[16]) which was imported from Australia to control the cottony-cushion scale (Icerya purchasi) in California. Some ladybugs, however, are pests themselves by directly feeding on crops. In the US two species are known to exhibit this lifestyle: the squash beetle (Epilachna borealis) and the Mexican bean beetle (Epilachna varivestis). The latter attacks beans and other legumes, while the former is a pest of squash and related commodities. Lastly, some ladybugs like the multicolored Asian lady beetle (Harmonia axyridis[17]), were imported for biocontrol of pests but end up becoming pests themselves, displacing native ladybugs and becoming a nuisance to homeowners when they infest houses (even being known to stain materials, smell badly, cause allergies and, in extreme cases, bite).How to identify Coccinellidae:Ladybugs generally range in size from 0.8-11mm.
