Galls on the juniper are often perennial and produce telia annually for many years, until the branch dies.  Occasionally the fungus will grow into a year old branch of the hawthorn or rosaceous host and create a canker. The fungus can overwinter in such cankers and produce aecia again the following spring.  More typically the infected tissue on the aecial host dies at the end of the growing season and a new infection is required for aecial production the next year.Geographic DistributionThe fungus is native to North America and occurs throughout the continent in both Canada and the United States. Aeciospores appear shrunken and irregular in shape when they are dry.Symptoms and SignsHawthorn twigs, fruits, petioles, thorns and leaves may be infected, with symptoms becoming visible about 7-10 days after  infection.  Fungal infection results in the production of abnormally large cells (hypertrophy), causing the host tissue to appear swollen.  Infected hawthorn twigs have spindle-shaped swellings.  Veins, a common infection site on leaves, swell and host the spermatial stage of the fungus.  The spermatial and aecial stages of the fungus are produced on infected twigs, fruits, petioles, and at the base of thorns.  Diseased leaves curl and die, but do not produce aecia.  Infected tissue typically dies after aecial production, resulting in twig dieback.Ecology and SpreadGymnosporangium clavipesis an obligate parasite that requires aJuniperussp. and rosaceous host to complete its lifecycle.Aecial hosts of the fungus include apple (Malussp.), chokeberry (Aroniasp.), flowering quince (Chaenomelessp.), hawthorn/mayhaw (Crataegussp.), mountain ash (Sorbussp.), pear (Pyrussp.), quince (Cydonia oblonga), serviceberry (Amelanchiersp.),Photinia,Mespilis, andCotoneaster.Juniperusspp. host the telial stage of the fungus.Aesciospores are released in the presence

The windborne aeciospores infect juniper leaves and green twigs in mid-summer to early fall.  In the spring of the following year, slightly swollen, spindle-shaped areas form on the infected juniper twigs and become covered in orange, cushion-like telia.  Teliospores germinate on the juniper host during wet conditions and produce basidiospores.  The basidiospores are windborne to the hawthorn host.  Galls on the juniper are often perennial and produce telia annually for many years, until the branch dies.  Occasionally the fungus will grow into a year old branch of the hawthorn or rosaceous host and create a canker. The fungus can overwinter in such cankers and produce aecia again the following spring.  More typically the infected tissue on the aecial host dies at the end of the growing season and a new infection is required for aecial production the next year.Geographic DistributionThe fungus is native to North America and occurs throughout the continent in both Canada and the United States.  The fungus has not been reported from anywhere else in the world.ManagementManagement of any pathogen is often dependent upon both cultural and chemical options.
