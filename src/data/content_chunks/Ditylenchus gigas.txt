Assessment of population densities of D. gigas developmental life stages in infected stem tissues of faba beans showed that the proportion of J4 increases approaching plant senescence and is almost 100% at plant maturity ( Vovlas et al., 2011 ). In contrast, adult stages were the least represented (1.1-8.5%) in all stem tissues. In any case, all developmental stages outside the egg are capable of infecting plants, but J4 is the most important infective stage due to its ability to withstand desiccation and to undergo anabiosis. Extensive, brown necrotic zones can be observed on the surface of faba bean stems towards the end of the crop season. Later, the tissue degradation extended to upper aboveground plant parts, affecting apical stems, leaves and even undersized pods and seeds. Diseased necrotic tissues displaying the characteristic symptoms contain juveniles, females, males and embryonated eggs of D. gigas . Examination of infected necrotic areas revealed internal necrosis colonized by nematodes, which induce the formation of sub-epidermic cavities surrounded by necrotic cells. Nematode migration and feeding in sub-epidermic cell layers dissolve cells, resulting in the enlargement of intercellular spaces. Ditylenchus gigas is very resilient, being able to endure sub-zero winter temperatures, warm summer soil temperatures of 55¬∞C and can survive for years in quiescent J4 stage form in plant debris, seed and soil.  Sintomas: Giant stem and bulb nematodes feed within the top plant parts and may infect seeds. D. gigas causes severe swellings and deformations of faba bean stem tissues or lesions which turn reddish brown then black. Newly formed pods turn dark brown. The lesions envelop the stem and increase in length (from 0.2 to 8 cm), often reaching the edge of an internode. In severely infected plants (84 to 14,945 nematodes per g of stem tissue can be found), leaf and petiole necrosis was also evident, which can be confused with symptoms induced by fungal leaf pathogens. In this advanced stage of infection, seeds, which were also infected by the nematode (11 to 816 nematodes per g of seed tissue can be detected) appear darker, distorted, smaller in size and with speckle-like spots on the surface. Heavy infections often kill the main shoots, stimulating secondary tiller formation ( Vovlas et al., 2011 ).
