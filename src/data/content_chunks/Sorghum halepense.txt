 Reproduccion: Genetics According to cytological, morphological and molecular data, S. halepense is a tetraploid species with a chromosome count of 2n = 40, arising as a natural hybrid between S. bicolor (2n=20), an annual African cultivated species and S. propinquum (2n=20), a perennial Asian species. S. halepense has a S. bicolor enriched allele composition and mutations in 5957 genes that differentiate it from its progenitors ( Paterson et al., 2020 ). There are a few reports of a tropical ecotype from Eurasia being diploid (2n=20) ( PPQ, 2022 ). Populations from the USA and Canada are of Mediterranean origin and are a tetraploid ecotype. Sorghum halepense can hybridize with S. bicolor (2n=20), and the progeny are usually sterile triploids which may be weedy in subsequent crops. Occasionally fertile hybrids occur, one of which is believed to be the origin of the fodder crop Columbus grass or black Sudan grass, S . x almum [ Sorghum almum ] (2n=40) in Argentina. Although hybridization with the crop sorghum is relatively rare and difficult to achieve in practice ( Dweikat, 2005 ), genetic studies by Morrell et al. (2005) suggest that there has been extensive introgression of genes from the crop into S. halepense in areas where the crop is commonly grown. Hybridization with auto-tetraploid sorghum is, however, relatively easy ( Liang et al., 1999 ). A study of populations of S. halepense in Kansas, USA showed high levels of genetic variability, suggesting that there is abundant reproduction from seed and only localized vegetative spread ( Camacho et al., 1993 ). SORGHUM HALEPENSE (L.) Pers. Family: Poaceae Synonyms: Andropogon halepensis (L.)Brot. Common names: Johnsongrass, Aleppo, Milletgrass, Sorgo de Alepo, Â  Zacate Johnson, Grama China, CaÃ±uela, Don Carlos, Sorgho d'Alep, Herbe de Cuba, Gumai. A tufted perennial grass with tall erect culms up to 2-2.5 m height . Its purplish inflorescence is an open panicle with dissimilar spikelets, 4-7 mm long. Plants develop long rhizomes bearing buds, which germinate readily. Rhizome growth of johnsongrass is more abundant than the shoot growth and in some cases rhizome fresh weight reaches 90% of the whole-developed johnsongrass plant. Apical dominance is broken with rhizome fragmentation, which consistently stimulates lateral bud germination and each bud produces one shoot. It is present in many countries of the world. A tufted perennial grass with tall erect culms up to 2-2.5 m height . Its purplish inflorescence is an open panicle with dissimilar spikelets, 4-7 mm long. Plants develop long rhizomes bearing buds, which germinate readily. It prevails in hot climatic conditions and tends to be more productive during the rainy season in tropical areas. Reproduction is by seed and rhizome production.
