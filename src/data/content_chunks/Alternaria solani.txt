Fruit infections may begin in either the green or ripe stage and appear as a sunken decay and are usually covered with a black mass of spores.Ecology and SpreadEarly blight is present in all tomato growing regions and causes damage wherever a humid climate or frequent dews create conditions for infection. The fungus survives on infected plant debris between crops, or on solanaceous weeds in warmer climates. Conidia are wind disseminated and generally infect during rainy or humid weather when temperatures are between 24-29 C. Lesions may develop 2 days after infection under favorable conditions. Infection is more severe on plants stressed poor fertility or other problems. Infection is heaviest on lower leaves first and defoliation moves from the bottom of the plant up. Lesion appear in largest numbers when plants are bearing fruit.ManagementEradicate weeds and volunteer solanaceous crop plantsUse only clean seed saved from disease-free plants.Practice crop rotation to non-susceptible crops (3 years). Be sure to control volunteers and susceptible weeds.Remove and destroy crop residue at the end of the season. Where this is not practical, plow residue into the soil to promote breakdown by soil microorganisms and to physically remove the spore source from the soil surface.Promote good air circulation by proper spacing of plants.Avoid overhead (sprinkler) irrigation.
