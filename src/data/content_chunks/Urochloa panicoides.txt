rochloa, Urochloa panicoides
 Invasion: U. panicoides is a tufted annual grass, with culms 6-60 cm high, sometimes decumbent and rooting from the lower nodes, usually with flowering branches. Leaves are usually loosely to densely hairy with tubercle-based hairs, blades up to 12 mm wide, expanded, light green, margins thickened and crinkled. Ligule a line of hairs 1 mm long. The inflorescence is up to 8 cm long, comprising 2-7 racemes up to 6 cm long; spikelets 4-5 mm long, somewhat flattened, solitary and almost sessile, forming two regular rows; glumes unequal, the lower one-quarter to one-third as long as the spikelet; upper lemma mucronate. Variety panicoides has glabrous spikelets, var. pubescens has pubescent spikelets.

Environmental RequirementsU. panicoides is a tropical to sub-tropical species, susceptible to both frost and very wet conditions, with optimum temperatures for growth of 25-40¬∞C. Seedlings of U. panicoides were generally associated with shaded habitats in Botswana surviving low light conditions, and the relative growth rate of seedlings was negatively related to seed weight ( Veenendaal et al., 1993 ). Nel et al. (1993) classified grass species on the red turfveld of the Springbok Flats Turf thornveld into ecological status groups according to their reaction to degradation, with U. panicoides included in the class 3 stage of degradation, representing the best indicator for severely grazed veld. Surveys by Awasthi et al. (1991) on the vegetation structure and composition of limestone quarries aged between 4 and 8 years situated in Madhya Pradesh, India found that U. panicoides was one of two dominant species at the top of the quarry in spring. Pandit et al. (1991) found that a grazing area in Gujarat, India in a semiarid coastal zone that had been protected from animals for 5 years was dominated by several species including U. panicoides. They also found that seasonal variation in the aboveground biomass corresponded with seasonal rainfall distribution.
