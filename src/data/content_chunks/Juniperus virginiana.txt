 Invasion: Juniperus virginianais a small evergreen, dioecious tree, commonly 20-37 m in height, and up to 100(-160) cm stem diameter. Crown on young trees is elongated ovate (cone shape) becoming rounder in age. Bark and branches grey to red-brown, thin, fibrous. Leaves are opposite, simple, green or blue-green, closely appressed and overlapping the leaf above, scale-like, 1-2 mm long with a sharp point. Male and female cones are on separate trees. Staminate (male) strobili or conelets begin to develop on male trees at the tips of axillary branches of new scale-leaves. Pollen grains are formed in conelets having 10-12 entire-margined sporophylls. Staminate strobili turn yellowish-brown when they reach maturity. The ovulate (female) cones are solitary at the tips of branchlets, dark blue or bluish. Fecundated cones are pale blue with whitish bloom, waxy and berrylike, 1 cm diameter, 1-4 seeds in each cone. Seeds are yellow-brown and round, 2-4 mm in diameter, ridged near the base, and sometimes shallowly pitted (Adams, 1993; 2000;USDA NRCS, 2002).

 Reproduccion: Genetics There is evidence that hybridization and considerable introgression occur in areas where Juniperus species overlap. Population studies suggest J. virginiana hybridizes with J. scopulorum , J. horizontalis and J. ashei ( Haverbeke, 1968 ; Adams and Turner, 1970 ; Flake et al., 1978 ). Hybridization between J. virginiana and J. bermudiana has been suggested to occur in Bermuda ( Adams and Wingate, 2008 ). Reproductive Biology Juniperus virginiana is a dioecious tree pollinated by wind and trees frequently reach sexual maturity when they are about 10 years old. When staminate conelets are mature, the microsporangial walls split longitudinally, discharging the mature pollen. Pollen grains lodge at the end of the micropyle of the many ovules in the female conelets. Pollination is complete in a few days when the conelet closes ( Lawson, 1990 ). Physiology and Phenology In North America, small green conelets begin to develop by late summer or early autumn on ovulate trees but grow very little during the winter. They are borne terminally on axillary branches of the new scale-leaves but do not become conspicuous until late February to early spring. Pollination occurs in early summer and the mature embryo is fully grown in about 2 months. Cones change colour from green to bluish as the season progresses. Most of the natural germination takes place in early spring of the second year after dispersal ( Lawson, 1990 ). Longevity

Sabina virginianaCommon Nameeastern redcedarGeneral Description:Eastern red cedar is an evergreen tree' native to eastern North America. Grown as an ornamental tree, and to a small extent for Christmas trees, it grows 5-20 m tall and up to 30 m in height.  It has typically an ovoid or pyramidal crown.J. virginianavar. crebra Fern. and Grisc. has a more columnar shape.[1]The red bark is thin (0.32 to 0.64 cm. thick) and exfoliates in long strips.[2]Leaves of seedlings and juvenile growth are acicular; adult plants have entire, opposite scale  like leaves that are tightly appressed and overlapping.  The scale like leaves have an oval dorsal gland.  The species is dioecous.[3]The blue black, often glaucous fruit is a fleshy berry like cone. Each fruit usually contains one to two seeds.[3]For further description see Rosendahl 1970, Fernald 1950.[4]NATURAL HISTORYRangeJ. virginianagrows in the eastern United States and adjacent Canada from Ontario to South Dakota, Nebraska, Kansas, eastern Texas, to north Florida, and north to Nova Scotia.[3]J. virginianais very susceptible to fire destruction.[5][6][2][7][8]It is a problem because it has rapidly invaded some grasslands and savannas mostly due to fire suppression.[9][8]In areas that once burned periodically, eastern red cedar was protected from fire on dry or rocky sites lacking sufficient herbaceous fuel to carry a fire.[10][8]As fire frequency decreased, eastern red cedar invaded adjacent, and apparently stable plant communities.[11]Subsequently, individual eastern red cedars have increased in size and coverage, and stand density has increased.  Invasion: Juniperus virginianais a small evergreen, dioecious tree, commonly 20-37 m in height, and up to 100(-160) cm stem diameter. Crown on young trees is elongated ovate (cone shape) becoming rounder in age. Bark and branches grey to red-brown, thin, fibrous. Leaves are opposite, simple, green or blue-green, closely appressed and overlapping the leaf above, scale-like, 1-2 mm long with a sharp point. Male and female cones are on separate trees. Staminate (male) strobili or conelets begin to develop on male trees at the tips of axillary branches of new scale-leaves. Pollen grains are formed in conelets having 10-12 entire-margined sporophylls. Staminate strobili turn yellowish-brown when they reach maturity. The ovulate (female) cones are solitary at the tips of branchlets, dark blue or bluish. Fecundated cones are pale blue with whitish bloom, waxy and berrylike, 1 cm diameter, 1-4 seeds in each cone. Seeds are yellow-brown and round, 2-4 mm in diameter, ridged near the base, and sometimes shallowly pitted (Adams, 1993; 2000;USDA NRCS, 2002).

 Reproduccion: Genetics There is evidence that hybridization and considerable introgression occur in areas where Juniperus species overlap. Population studies suggest J. virginiana hybridizes with J. scopulorum , J. horizontalis and J. ashei ( Haverbeke, 1968 ; Adams and Turner, 1970 ; Flake et al., 1978 ). Hybridization between J. virginiana and J. bermudiana has been suggested to occur in Bermuda ( Adams and Wingate, 2008 ). Reproductive Biology Juniperus virginiana is a dioecious tree pollinated by wind and trees frequently reach sexual maturity when they are about 10 years old. When staminate conelets are mature, the microsporangial walls split longitudinally, discharging the mature pollen. Pollen grains lodge at the end of the micropyle of the many ovules in the female conelets. Pollination is complete in a few days when the conelet closes ( Lawson, 1990 ). Physiology and Phenology In North America, small green conelets begin to develop by late summer or early autumn on ovulate trees but grow very little during the winter. They are borne terminally on axillary branches of the new scale-leaves but do not become conspicuous until late February to early spring. Pollination occurs in early summer and the mature embryo is fully grown in about 2 months. Cones change colour from green to bluish as the season progresses. Most of the natural germination takes place in early spring of the second year after dispersal ( Lawson, 1990 ). Longevity

Sabina virginianaCommon Nameeastern redcedarGeneral Description:Eastern red cedar is an evergreen tree' native to eastern North America. Grown as an ornamental tree, and to a small extent for Christmas trees, it grows 5-20 m tall and up to 30 m in height.  It has typically an ovoid or pyramidal crown.J. virginianavar. crebra Fern. and Grisc. has a more columnar shape.[1]The red bark is thin (0.32 to 0.64 cm. thick) and exfoliates in long strips.[2]Leaves of seedlings and juvenile growth are acicular; adult plants have entire, opposite scale  like leaves that are tightly appressed and overlapping.  The scale like leaves have an oval dorsal gland.  The species is dioecous.[3]The blue black, often glaucous fruit is a fleshy berry like cone.
