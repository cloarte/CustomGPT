It is also known as bladder campion, white campion, and evening lychnis. White cockle is 1.5 to 3.5 feet tall and has jointed stems that can be spreading or nearly erect. The plant is hairy below and glandular toward the top. Its hairy leaves are opposite, simple, and ovate to lanceolate. The plant is dioecious, with male and female flowers borne on separate plants. The fragrant flowers are solitary and are found on long peduncles or in cymose clusters. The flowers have five white or pink deeply notched petals greatly exceeding the length of the calyx. The flowers open in the evening and close by noon. The small, rounded, rough seeds are produced in a bulb-like capsule.HabitatsWhite cockle may be found in grasslands and new clover and alfalfa seedings. In dry climates, it is typically found on irrigated land. Seeds germinate primarily in the fall, but germination can occur anytime the soil is warm and moist. Seedlings are intolerant of high temperatures. Flowering begins in May and continues through October. Seeds are very light and can be spread through recycled irrigation water. Although the seeds can remain viable for several years in the soil, the seed bank on the surface normally lasts about three years. It is also known as bladder campion, white campion, and evening lychnis. White cockle is 1.5 to 3.5 feet tall and has jointed stems that can be spreading or nearly erect. The plant is hairy below and glandular toward the top. Its hairy leaves are opposite, simple, and ovate to lanceolate. The plant is dioecious, with male and female flowers borne on separate plants. The fragrant flowers are solitary and are found on long peduncles or in cymose clusters. The flowers have five white or pink deeply notched petals greatly exceeding the length of the calyx. The flowers open in the evening and close by noon. The small, rounded, rough seeds are produced in a bulb-like capsule.HabitatsWhite cockle may be found in grasslands and new clover and alfalfa seedings. While fields, roadsides, pastures, grass embankments, and waste places are characteristic white cockle habitats, it is also found in many undisturbed areas. This species prefers well-drained soils.ImpactsWhite cockle can be a serious problem in small grains, alfalfa, clover, and grass seed fields. Due to similarity in size, the seeds are difficult to separate from commercially produced clover or alfalfa seed.Biology and EcologyWhite cockle reproduces by seeds and short rootstocks. In dry climates, it is typically found on irrigated land. Seeds germinate primarily in the fall, but germination can occur anytime the soil is warm and moist. Seedlings are intolerant of high temperatures. Flowering begins in May and continues through October. Seeds are very light and can be spread through recycled irrigation water. Although the seeds can remain viable for several years in the soil, the seed bank on the surface normally lasts about three years. A single white campion plant is capable of producing up to 25,000 seeds per season.Management ApproachesBiological ControlThere are no biological control agents available for control of white cockle.Mechanical and Cultural ControlCultivation or tillage for regular seedbed preparation will kill many white cockle plants. Well-established plants are usually not killed and tillage can promote seed germination. Tillage can be a suitable control option in row crops or prior to seeding small-seeded grasses or legumes.
