Reproductive Biology Solanum torvum is andromonoecious, producing inflorescences with both hermaphroditic flowers and functionally staminate flowers, the latter situated on the distal portion of the inflorescence. In hermaphroditic flowers, which represent 80-85 % of all flowers, the style extends for about 2-2.5 mm beyond the anther cones, whereas in the functionally staminate flowers the style does not protrude from the anther cones ( Hossain, 1973 ; Liu and Pemberton, 2009 ). Overview
Appearance
Solanum torvum
Turkey berry is a broadleaved, evergreen, shrub or small tree that invades a variety of ecosystems in Florida. Plants can grow to 16 ft. (4.9 m) in height. The stems are armed with stout, straight or lightly curved prickles.
Foliage
The alternate leaves are elliptical. Leaves are up to 10 in. (25 cm) long, 3 in. (7 cm) wide, wavy along the margins and have prickles on the veins.
Flowers
Solanum torvum
has small, white flowers that occur in large, branched clusters. Plants flower continuously after reaching a height of 3.3-4.9 ft. (1-1.5 m).
Fruit
The fruits are small green berries that turn yellow when ripe.
Ecological Threat
Although
Solanum torvum
has only been found several times in Florida, it has the potential to invade a variety of sites, both wet and dry. Once established, it can sprout from the roots, creating large thickets that could displace native vegetation.

Reproductive Biology Solanum torvum is andromonoecious, producing inflorescences with both hermaphroditic flowers and functionally staminate flowers, the latter situated on the distal portion of the inflorescence. In hermaphroditic flowers, which represent 80-85 % of all flowers, the style extends for about 2-2.5 mm beyond the anther cones, whereas in the functionally staminate flowers the style does not protrude from the anther cones ( Hossain, 1973 ; Liu and Pemberton, 2009 ). The flowers are nectarless and typically last only 1 day, although sometimes they may open for a second day ( Hossain, 1973 ). They are visited and pollinated by matinal and diurnal bees that "buzz" the pollen out of the poricidal anthers. These include carpenter bees ( Xylocopa spp.), bumblebees ( Bombus spp.), sweat bees ( Augochlorella spp., Augochloropsis spp., Lasioglossum spp., Megalopta spp.) and orchid bees ( Euglossa spp., Eulaema spp.) ( Janzen, 1968 ; Liu and Pemberton, 2009 ; Staines et al., 2017 ). No fruits are produced when bees are excluded, demonstrating the dependency of S. torvum on these pollinators for reproduction ( Liu and Pemberton, 2009 ). Turkey berry is a prolific fruit and seed producer ( Welman, 2003 ). About 70-75% percent of long-styled or hermaphroditic flowers will produce fruits. Depending on the size, a single fruit can contain between 175 and 400 small seeds ( Hossain, 1973 ; Symon, 1981 ), which are spread by frugivorous birds and mammals. Physiology and Phenology Solanum torvum is a short-lived perennial growing about 0.75 to 1.5 m per year. Plants start producing flowers after 3-4 months or once they reach 1 to 1.5 m in height. They can live for up to 5 years ( Schippers, 2004 ) but most live for about 2 years ( Francis, 2004 ). Associations Solanum torvum is a major pollen source for bees on which it depends for reproduction ( Janzen 1966 ; 1968 ; Liu and Pemberton, 2009 ; Staines et al., 2017 ). The fruits are a food source for birds ( Stocker and Irvine, 1983 ; Corlett, 2005 ), bats ( Lobova and Mori, 2004 ), primates ( Sabater-Pi, 1977 ) and ruminants ( Gautier-Hion et al., 1980 ). Overview
Appearance
Solanum torvum
Turkey berry is a broadleaved, evergreen, shrub or small tree that invades a variety of ecosystems in Florida. Plants can grow to 16 ft. (4.9 m) in height. The stems are armed with stout, straight or lightly curved prickles.
Foliage
The alternate leaves are elliptical. Leaves are up to 10 in. (25 cm) long, 3 in. (7 cm) wide, wavy along the margins and have prickles on the veins.
Flowers
Solanum torvum
has small, white flowers that occur in large, branched clusters. Plants flower continuously after reaching a height of 3.3-4.9 ft. (1-1.5 m).
Fruit
The fruits are small green berries that turn yellow when ripe.
Ecological Threat
Although
Solanum torvum
has only been found several times in Florida, it has the potential to invade a variety of sites, both wet and dry. Once established, it can sprout from the roots, creating large thickets that could displace native vegetation.
