 Reproduccion: The biology of T. fuscicornis is only partially known. However, general descriptions of the biology of siricids are available ( Smith and Schiff, 2002 ). These insects live in true symbiosis with basidiomycete fungi, which are capable of degrading lignin, concentrating nutritive elements in their mycelia, which are scarce in wood, and serve as food for the larvae. These feed and develop inside the tree, compromising the heartwood, until reaching pupation. The mitochondrial genome of T. fuscicornis was published by Park et al. (2020) , who also found the taxon to be phylogenetically well-supported. Adult wasps often mate in the upper branches of trees ( USDA-APHIS, 2011 ). Females then select stressed or injured trees to begin egg laying. A description of the oviposition and hatching behaviour can be found in Suttrop (2006) . The females will lay 300-400 eggs and will usually die in oviposition ( Isebrands and Richardson, 2014 ). Eggs are placed perpendicularly and at an oblique angle, so that they are separate in the wood, but grouped together. The sex ratio is usually 1:1 ( Isebrands and Richardson, 2014 ). When the first-instar larvae emerge, they exclusively feed on the hyphae of the developing symbiont fungus. Later the larvae construct longitudinal, semi-circular galleries, which can run either upwards or downwards. The total length of a gallery is approximately 1 m and it gradually increases in diameter as the larvae grow. Pupation occurs inside the wood at a depth of approximately 4 cm from the bark surface.
